How an Offset Account Works

The simplest wealth-building tool most Australians underuse.

1

Your savings sit in offset

Instead of a regular savings account, your money lives in an offset account linked to your mortgage.

2

Interest calculated on the difference

The bank only charges interest on your loan balance minus your offset balance — saving you money every single day.

3

More of each payment hits principal

Less interest means more of every repayment reduces your actual loan — compounding your savings over time.

Frequently Asked Questions

The questions we get asked most.

Should I pay a lump sum off my mortgage or keep it in offset?
Keep it in the offset. The interest saving is identical, but the offset gives you full access to your money if an emergency arises. Once you pay a lump sum off the loan, it's locked away and you'd need to refinance to access it again.
What happens when my offset balance equals my loan balance?
You pay zero interest. Every single dollar of your monthly repayment goes directly to reducing the principal. At this point you can either continue repayments to pay it off, or withdraw from the offset to pay the loan in full and close the mortgage.
Is the interest saved on an offset account tax-free?
Yes — and this is one of the biggest advantages. You're not earning interest (which would be taxable), you're simply reducing the interest you're charged. The saving is effectively tax-free, making the offset equivalent rate much higher than a standard savings account after tax.
How much extra should I pay per month to pay off faster?
Use the calculator above. For a $695k loan at 6.20% with $148k in offset, increasing repayments from $4,160 to $5,000/month cuts roughly 10 years off the loan. Even an extra $200–$500/month makes a significant difference over 20 years.
Does this calculator work for all Australian lenders?
Yes — the calculator uses standard principal and interest amortization with an offset deduction, which applies to all major Australian lenders including CBA, ANZ, Westpac, NAB, Macquarie, and others. Always verify with your specific lender for exact figures.
